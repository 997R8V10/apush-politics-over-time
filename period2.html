1607-1757: Colonial North America

From Jamestown to the French and Indian War

Posted by Prithvi Shivaraman on September 25, 2016

An Image of the Era

This image perfectly illustrates the struggle for power between poor white farmers and rich aristocrats. Throughout the colonies, many class conflicts occured before the French and Indian War. Due to indentured servitude, many poor British men came over to the colonies and worked for several years. After they finished their servitude, they did not recieve housing or clothing, and instead went westward to establish their own settlement. One of such settlements, was in Virginia. A white farmer named Nathaniel Bacon lived in Western Virginia, and his community was continually harrased by Native Americans. The current governer of Virginia (Governer Berkeley), did not take any action and this angered many lower class colonists.

The Burning of Jamestown During Bacon's Rebellion.

Eventually Bacon and his followers decided to take action, and attacked innocent tribes. This actually ended up hurting Governer Berkeley's fur trade operation and he ordered Bacon to stop. Angered by this, Bacon and his followers marched on Jamestown. They burned the entire city to the ground and Berkeley and his forces retreated. Fortunately for Governer Berkeley, Bacon suddenley died due to health reasons, and Berkeley was able to take back the ruined city. This is one of the more famous examples of the early colonial power struggle between different classes and in some ways, caused major changes to ensure that such an event couldn't happen again. In particular, racism was used to united the white colonists against their black slaves.

Synthesis Article

Nathaniel Bacon's rebellion shows us what happens when class tensions become extreme, and one class is treated unfairly over another. However to remedy this problem, colonial administration simply solved the problem by putting more emphasis on racial divides. This still impacts America as a country today, after 400 years. Even after the civil war, racial tensions still existed throughout the 20th and now into the 21st century. In some ways it is becoming worse. Today's Black Lives Matter movement shows how racial tensions can quickly turn into protest, and sometimes violence. Sometimes these movements have no legitement purpose. A lot of times, the system is truly unfair, such as the equality and civil rights movement in the 1960s, which was spearheaded by African Americans. With the United States' influx of immigrants, it's hard to unite the country as a whole, and the problem has since expanded from just African Americans to several races. However, despite what many people think, racism and inequality has gone down severely since the Civil Rights Movement and the US has become much more open to immigration and different races. In the time of Bacon's rebellion, most white settlers were very racist towards even Europeans, let alone Africans. But despite the decrease in racism over the centuries, the problem still exists, since even the most disliked men are successful in capturing just under half of the United States population through racism, and white supremacy. This shows the inherent similarty throughout American history, of division, but also diversity. Diversity is a positive thing, but causes issues that must be overcome.

Conflicting Sources

Life in Virginia was clearly illustrated by Sebastian Brandt as dreary and very unpleasant. Government wasn't even possible, since many people perished due to "sicknesse & weakenesse" (Brandt). Conversely, in Boston, the Puritans were living a much better lifestyle. They were able to create a "civil Government" (Winthrop) due to the stability of the population. The nearby natives were not major threats to the Puritans, due to disease. Life in New England allowed for people to think about a just and equal government, as opposed to Virginia, where it grew as a more aristocratic society, due to the harshness of the land. In Virginia, the settlers must recieve food from England, because they cannot find adequate food on their own. New England was very adept at generating their own food supply and being very self sufficient, which also allowed for greater autonomy until the French and Indian War. Jamestown, on the other hand, had to be taken over by the government due to the Virginia Company's apparent failure to maintain the colony, and the lack of revenue produced. It was not until John Rolfe brought Tobacco to Jamestown, that it became a viable and somewhat prosperous settlement, but even so, had significant racial and class divides and a much weaker government during the era. The main reason for these major differences is the purpose of the settlements. Whereas New England was founded for mostly religious regions (as can be seen by Winthrops' constant references to God), Virginia was founded as a revenue generating colony. As a result, women rarely came over to Virginia except to be a worker in the fields, and this resulted in very low birth rates in Virginia. This, combined with swampy conditions and diseases, caused Virginians' to face many obstacles, and struggle to survive. New England's climate was much more temperate, and had many more families, which ensured a stable birth rate. The lack of disease baring mosquitoes, meant that the mortality rate was low as well, allowing for a stable government.

Well beloved good friend Henry Hovener
My comendations remembred, I hartely [wish] your welfare for god be thanked I am now in good health, but my brother and my wyfe are dead aboute a yeare pass’d And touchinge the busynesse that I came hither is nothing yett performed, by reason of my sicknesse & weaknesse I was not able to travell up and downe the hills and dales of these countries but doo nowe intend every daye to walke up and downe the hills for good Mineralls here is both golde silver and copper to be had and therefore I will doe my endeavour by the grace of god to effect what I am able to performe And I intreat you to beseeche the Right Hon: & Wor: Company in my behalfe to grant me my freedome to be sent either to me I dowbte not to doo well & good service in these countries humbly desyringe them also to provyde me some [appointed] fellowe & a strong boye to assiste me in my businesse, and that it may please the aforesaid Company to send me at my charge a bed wth a bolster and cover and some Linnen for shirtes and sheetes. Sixe fallinge bands wth Last Size pairs of shoes twoo pairs of bootes three pairs of cullered stockings and garters wth three pairs of lether gloves some powder and shott twoo little runletts of oyle and vinnegar some spice & suger to comfort us here in our sicknesse abowte ffyftie pounds weight of holland and Englishe cheese together, Lykewyse some knyves, spoons, combes and all sorts of cullerd beads as you knowe the savage Indians use Allso one Rundlett wth all sortes of yron nayles great and small, three haire sives, two hatchetts wth twoo broad yrons and some Allum And send all these necessaries thinges in a dry fatt wth the first shippinge dyrected unto Mr. Pontes in James Towne here in Virginia And whatsoever this all costes I will not onely wth my moste humble service but allso wth some good Tobacco Bevor and Otterskins and other commodities here to be had recompence the Company for the same And yf you could send for my brother Phillipps Sonne in Darbesheere to come hether itt [were] a great commoditie ffor me or suche another used in minerall workes And thus I comitt you to the Almighty. Virginia 13 January 1622.
Sebastian Brandt, 1622

Worthye Sr
That you are pleased among yr many & weighty imployments to spend so many serious thoughts and good wishes upon us, & the work of the Lord in our hands, I must needs acknowledge it among other the special favours of God towards us, and an undoubted testimony of yr sincere Love towards us: which makes me the more careful to satisfy yr desire, of being truly informed of our estate (this being the first safe means of Conveyance since I received yrs in October last) you may please therefore to understand that first, for the number of our people, we never took any survey of them, nor do we intend it, except inforced through urgent occasion (David’s example sticks somewhat with us) [some Protestants interpreted the Bible as forbidding a census] but I esteem them to be in all about 4000 souls & upward: in good health (for the most parse) & well provided of all necessarys: so as (through the Lords special providence) there hath not died about 2: or 3: grown persons, & about so many Children all in the last year, it being verye rare to heare of any sick of agues or other diseases, nor have I known of any quartan Ague amonge us since I came into the Countrye. For Our susistence here, the means hitherto hath been the yearly access of new Comers, who have supplied all our wants, for Cattle, & the fruits of our labours, as board, pale, smiths work etc: if this should fail, then we have other meanes which may supply us, as fish viz: Cod, bass & herring, for which no place in the world exceeds us, if we can compass salt at a reasonable rate: our grounds likewise are apt for hemp & flax & rape seeds, & all sorts of roots, pumpkins & other fruits, which for taste & wholesomeness far exceed those in England: our grapes also (wherewith the Country abounds) afford a good hard wine. Our ploughs go on with good success, we are like to have 20 at work next year: our lands are aptest for Rye and oats. Our winters are sharp & longe, I may reckon 4 months for storing of cattle, but we find no difference whither they be housed or go abroad: our summers are somewhat more fervent in heat than in England. Our civil Government is mixt: the freemen choose the magistrates every year . . . and at 4: courts in the year 3: out of each town (there being 8 in all) do assist the magistrates in making of laws, imposing taxes, & disposing of lands: our furies [?] are chosen by the freemen of everye town. Our Churches are governed by Pastors, Teachers ruling Elders & Deacons, yet the power lies in the whole Congregation and not in the Presbytery [not in a larger council of churches] further than for order and precedence. For the natives, they are near all dead of the smallpox, so the Lord hath cleared our title to what we possess.
John Winthrop, 1636
