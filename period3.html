1758-1800: A New Nation

The American Revolution and Constitution

Posted by Prithvi Shivaraman on October 17, 2016

An Image of the Era

Howard C. Christy's Scene at the Signing of the Constitution of the United States.

Synthesis Article

In the years after the revolution, the United States, than only an infant country, was under the Articles of Confederation. The Articles created a system of government which was very loose, and resulted in a very weak central government. After repeated problems, the country quickly realized that the new system was very inefficient, and left the young country very vunerable to outside attacks. In 1787, after Shay's rebellion in Massachussetts, several delegates met in Philadelphia to ammend the Articles. However, rather than just amending the Articles, they replaced it entirely with a new Constitution. These course of events show how the United States is ready to adapt to any situation. Furthermore, the Constitution was designed in a way that would allow for the document to be adapted to fit the needs of the time period. For example, the 13th amendment made sure that slaves were emancipated in order to fit the morals of a new time period. Just a century earlier, it was considered moral to own a slave. In fact, the constitution has been amended over 30 times, and is the longest surviving document to govern a country.

Today, however, there is a reluctance to amend the constitution. The biggest example of this is the 2nd amendment, which guarantees every citizen the right to bear arms. Unfortunately, this was written in the late 1700s, when the most powerful guns were muskets. Today, weapons can fire over 10 rounds every second, which is excessive for the average citizen. Despite attempts to amend this amendment, lobbyists such as the NRA (National Rifle Association) have repeatedly rejected the proposal referencing the 2nd amendment. Amendments in the past, however, have been repealed and amended; such as the Prohibition amendment, which was quickly repealed when the population realized it was having no effect. Despite this issues, it must be remembered that most amendments have always had a lot of debate before going through, as is the tradition of the United States. President Lincoln fought an entire war over the 13th amendment to get it recognized in the South. In the US, a debate is always considered healthy, as it allows the country to consider different points of view before making a major decision.
