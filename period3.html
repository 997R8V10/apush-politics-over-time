1758-1800: A New Nation

The American Revolution to the Constitution

Posted by Prithvi Shivaraman on October 17, 2016

An Image of the Era

The signing of the Constitution marks a significant event during the 3rd period of American History. The Constitution was a document that had to live up to enormous expectations. First, the Constitution had to be vague enough to be valid for several centuries, not a very easy task. Many other constitutions had to be amended several times, or even completely replaced very often due to incompatibilities. However, the constitution's basic principles have survived from 1788 to present, with only minor amendments. The image shows the many delagates, finally agreeing on the finalized version of the constitution, which took many debates and edits over the course of 4 months.

Howard C. Christy's Scene at the Signing of the Constitution of the United States.

The constitution also had to be specific enough as to avoid the citizenry from finding loopholes. The founding fathers created a specific structure of government that is still in practice to this day, and took almost every factor into consideration. This image perfectly represents the period, since it captures the struggle to create and perfect a new system of government, which occured throughout the period. The government was edited, amendments were passed, and it was a time of mostly trial-and-error.

Synthesis Article

In the years after the revolution, the United States, than only an infant country, was under the Articles of Confederation. The Articles created a system of government which was very loose, and resulted in a very weak central government. After repeated problems, the country quickly realized that the new system was very inefficient, and left the young country very vunerable to outside attacks. In 1787, after Shay's rebellion in Massachussetts, several delegates met in Philadelphia to amend the Articles. However, rather than just amending the Articles, they replaced it entirely with a new Constitution. These course of events show how the United States is ready to adapt to any situation. Furthermore, the Constitution was designed in a way that would allow for the document to be adapted to fit the needs of the time period. For example, the 13th amendment made sure that slaves were emancipated in order to fit the morals of a new time period. Just a century earlier, it was considered moral to own a slave. In fact, the constitution has been amended over 30 times, and is the longest surviving document to govern a country.

Today, however, there is a reluctance to amend the constitution. The biggest example of this is the 2nd amendment, which guarantees every citizen the right to bear arms. Unfortunately, this was written in the late 1700s, when the most powerful guns were muskets. Today, weapons can fire over 10 rounds every second, which is excessive for the average citizen. Despite attempts to amend this amendment, lobbyists such as the NRA (National Rifle Association) have repeatedly rejected the proposal referencing the 2nd amendment. Amendments in the past, however, have been repealed and amended; such as the Prohibition amendment, which was quickly repealed when the population realized it was having no effect. Despite this issues, it must be remembered that most amendments have always had a lot of debate before going through, as is the tradition of the United States. President Lincoln fought an entire war over the 13th amendment to get it recognized in the South. In the US, a debate is always considered healthy, as it allows the country to consider different points of view before making a major decision.

Continuity Over Time

Although much changed between the colonial era and the revolutionary era, there were still many commonatlities. Although the Articles of Confederation was created to be a total opposite of the British Empire, however, this quickly fell apart. The resulting system of government was very similar to the British bicameral system of legislature, with one ruler. States still retained their local legislatures which were relatively similar to their system of government before the election. However, many things changed between the two eras. For example, Americans moved away from the idea of a monarchy to a democracy after the French and Indian war. Americans also believed that the populus should get equal representation in Parliament, which led to the breakout of the American revolution. Furthermore, Americans had revolutionary ideas which completely differed from Europe, and actually served as a model for European countries such as France and eventually Britain. Americans also embraced older European ideas such as the unalieanable rights from John Locke during the Enlightenment.

Conflicting Sources

In the lead-up to the American Revolution, both Britain and the colonies had separate views of the disastrous situation in the colonies. After the French and Indian War, Britain was under a lot of debt, and needed to pay it off. "And We do further strictly enjoin and require all Persons whatever, who have either wilfully or inadvertently seated themselves upon any Lands within the Countries above described, or upon any other Lands, which, not having been ceded to, or purchased by Us, are still reserved to the said Indians as aforesaid, forthwith to remove themselves from such Settlements." (George, 1763). One of the ways they accomplished this was by preventing the Americans from crossing the frontier. Furthermore, Britain imposed several taxes on the colonials, which destroyed the American economy. This made the colonists very upset and they started harrasing several British officials. King George, however saw this as malicious and this led to the port of Boston being closed. In the second pictures, the British depict the sons of liberty "Tarring and Feathering" a British official with no mercy. The colonists felt that they should not be taxed, without recieving adequate representation. However, Britain felt that the colonists were being represented "virtually". This major difference in point of view resulted mainly from the geographical difference of the two parties: One being in Britain, and one in the colonies. Life in the colonies was very different from life in Britain. However, the British saw the colonists as unruly and felt they were right. The colonists felt as though the British were being tyrannic. These severely differences in point-of-view was one of the many factors that led to the eventual independence of the United States from Great Britain.
